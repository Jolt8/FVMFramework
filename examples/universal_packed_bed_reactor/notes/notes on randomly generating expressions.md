Independent Variables / Physical Parameters: cp, k, rho, mu, g, D_p, L, epsilon #physical parameters (with units)

I wonder if better guesses could be generated by a machine learning algorithim that is trained off of already existing empirical correlations and physics expressions

Perhaps units could also aid in this process if we're not going completely empirical

I think we could achieve this by modelling expressions as trees and then have a model that's given the input: 
    - output units
    - output magnitude
    - physical parameters magnitude
    - avaliable physical parameters (like specific heat capacity, density, reynolds number, prandtl number, )
    - etc.
And outputs:
    - probable next operation
    - probable next occurance of an empirical constant
    - probable next occurance of a physical parameter
    - units of next coefficient
    - etc.

This training could even be reinforced by feeding back in the top 10 human chosen equations that were the simplest that also resulted in the best fit

If we could pull this off, we could have a model that could automatically generate simple-looking empirical correlations that would typically 
take years to create with very simple hardware which would be insanely valuable

I think a good way to test this would be to see if it could come up with the reynolds number formula by fitting it to data generated from the original formula

I wonder if developing such a thing would even be worth it. I mean, there's two ends of the spectrum:
    - when simulation times are LOW AND when you expect the final equation to be SIMPLE, you can just throw shit at a wall randomly and eventually you'll get a good equation 
    - when simulation times are LOW AND when you expect the final equation to be COMPLEX, you could probably still just throw shit at the wall and push it towards more complex expressions but a machine learning algorithim might be necessary (and this would probably be its best use case)
    - when simulation times are HIGH AND when you expect the final equation to be SIMPLE, manually entering in equations that seem suitable would probably work with some variations on those equations
    - when simulation times are HIGH AND when you expect the final equation to be COMPLEX, then machine learning would be very valuable

I wonder if LLMs would be at all practical for this application due to the difficulty of training my own machine learning model
    - Models like Gemini 3.1 Flash lite or maybe models that can be run at home would be useful as they could (very loosely!) pull from knowledge of already existing physics and years of engineering experience
